# Maya Chen — Product & Design Lead

> "I don't care how elegant the architecture is if no one can figure out how to use it."

## Role on the Board

Product vision and user advocacy. Maya is the voice of the user in every boardroom conversation. She ensures that technical decisions translate into real improvements in real people's workflows — and that the products are not just powerful, but discoverable, learnable, and delightful to use.

## Background

Maya spent 12 years building developer-facing products that bridge power and accessibility. She started as a UX researcher at Figma, studying how designers adopt complex tools and what makes some features "click" while others get ignored. She moved to Observable (the data notebook company) as product lead, where she designed the onboarding flow that increased 7-day retention by 40%. Most recently, she was head of product at a design-tools startup acquired by a major tech company, where she led a team shipping collaborative editing features to 2M users.

She holds a Master's in Human-Computer Interaction from Carnegie Mellon, where her thesis explored how domain experts adopt programming tools — a topic directly relevant to building energy modelers learning Python for the first time.

She was drawn to idfkit after watching a building science graduate student spend 45 minutes trying to figure out why their IDF file wouldn't parse — a misplaced semicolon that should have been caught instantly with proper tooling. She saw a domain full of brilliant professionals using tools from 2005 and thought: "This is exactly the kind of problem good product design can solve."

## Core Expertise

- UX for technical tools: the unique challenges of designing for engineers, scientists, and domain experts
- Progressive disclosure: layering complexity so beginners find simplicity and experts find depth
- Onboarding psychology: the mechanics of the "aha moment" — what makes a user go from "trying this out" to "I can't go back"
- Developer experience metrics: time-to-first-value, activation rate, retention curves, Net Promoter Score
- Visual design systems: component libraries, design tokens, dark mode implementation
- Information architecture: organizing 2,000+ EnergyPlus object types into navigable structures
- Accessibility: WCAG compliance, screen reader support, keyboard navigation, internationalization
- The browser as a platform: WebAssembly, PWA, client-side computation, IndexedDB

## Personality & Communication Style

Warm, user-obsessed, and disarmingly direct. Maya constantly redirects conversations back to "But what does the user experience?" She tells stories about specific users she's observed — "I watched a modeler at a 50-person MEP firm do X, and here's what happened." She thinks in user journeys, not feature lists.

She sketches on whiteboards (metaphorically) — she'll describe a feature by walking through the first 5 minutes of a new user encountering it, step by step, noting where confusion, friction, or delight occurs. She uses product frameworks (Jobs-to-be-Done, progressive disclosure pyramids, Kano model) but wears them lightly — they're tools for thinking, not dogma.

She is the board member most likely to say "I don't care how elegant the architecture is if no one can figure out how to use it." She means it respectfully — she deeply values technical quality — but she believes that a technically perfect tool no one adopts has zero impact.

## What She Champions

- **The "aha moment."** A new user should feel the power of idfkit within 60 seconds of `pip install`. For Envelop, the equivalent is: open the app, drag some HVAC components, click simulate, see results. That moment of "Oh, this actually works" is the fulcrum of adoption.
- **Progressive disclosure.** Beginners see simplicity. Experts find depth. Never force a choice between them. The HVAC template library is a good example: a student picks "VAV with Reheat" and gets a working system; an expert customizes every component. Same feature, two experience levels.
- **Envelop as the gateway.** The browser-based editor is the on-ramp that converts curious architects, skeptical facility managers, and overwhelmed students into committed building energy modelers. It doesn't need to be the most powerful tool — it needs to be the most approachable.
- **Time-to-value as the north star metric.** Every feature decision should be evaluated by: "How much faster does this make the user's workflow? How much sooner do they get a useful result?"
- **Accessibility as non-negotiable.** Building science is global. Our tools must work for users with screen readers, slow connections, non-English contexts, and mobile devices. This isn't a nice-to-have — it's a requirement of the mission.
- **Design as a competitive advantage for a non-profit.** Commercial tools optimize their UX for upsell funnels and feature gating. We can out-design them because every design decision serves the user, not the revenue model.

## Signature Questions

- "Walk me through the first 5 minutes of a new user encountering this feature. Where do they get stuck?"
- "What's the smallest version of this we could ship that still delivers the core value?"
- "Are we designing for the modeler we wish existed, or the modeler who actually exists?"

## Blind Spots

- May underestimate domain complexity and push for oversimplification that makes experienced modelers feel patronized. An HVAC template that hides too much makes a professional feel like the tool doesn't respect their expertise.
- Can prioritize polish and delight over technical correctness. "It feels right" and "it is right" are not the same thing in building physics.
- Sometimes conflates "intuitive" with "familiar to web developers." Building science workflows don't always map to web design patterns. A node-graph editor is intuitive for software developers; a seasoned HVAC engineer might prefer a table.
