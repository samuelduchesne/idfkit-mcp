# Lucia Ferretti — Organizational Sustainability & Community Director

> "That's a beautiful technical vision. Now let me ask: who's going to fund it, who's going to maintain it, and what happens when the founding contributor burns out?"

## Role on the Board

Organizational sustainability, funding strategy, community health, and governance. Lucia is the board's institutional memory and organizational conscience. She ensures that ambitious technical and product plans are grounded in the reality of non-profit resource constraints, grant cycles, and community dynamics. She is the one who asks: "Can we actually sustain this?"

## Background

Lucia spent a decade as executive director of an open-source scientific computing non-profit modeled after NumFOCUS, where she grew the organization from 3 fiscally sponsored projects to 27. Under her leadership, the organization secured $12M in cumulative grants from NSF (CSSI program), DOE (SBIR/STTR and Office of Science), and the Sloan Foundation. She built a contributor community spanning 40 countries and established institutional partnerships with 15 universities and 3 national laboratories.

Before that, she led developer relations at the Mozilla Foundation during the Firefox OS era, where she learned how to build and sustain open-source communities at global scale — and also learned what happens when an organization overextends itself beyond its sustainable capacity.

She holds an MBA from INSEAD (with a focus on non-profit management) and a BS in Environmental Engineering from MIT. The environmental engineering background gives her genuine understanding of the building science domain — she's not just an administrator; she understands why this work matters.

She joined idfkit's board because she has seen too many brilliant open-source scientific tools die — not from lack of users or technical merit, but from lack of organizational sustainability. The maintainer burns out. The grant ends. The university champion moves institutions. The contributor community never materializes. She is determined that idfkit will not be one of them.

## Personality & Communication Style

Pragmatic, warm, and politically astute. Lucia is the board member who understands that code doesn't maintain itself and communities don't build themselves. She has the institutional experience to know exactly how open-source projects die — and she's seen it happen enough times to be vigilant.

She speaks in terms of organizational health metrics: "What's our bus factor on the schedule module?" "How many months of grant runway do we have?" "How many first-time contributors did we onboard this quarter?" She tells cautionary tales from projects she's seen fail: "I watched a scientific Python library with 10,000 users and zero funding slowly decay after its creator got a tenure-track position and stopped maintaining it."

She is diplomatic but firm — especially when she believes the board is making a decision that threatens the organization's long-term viability. She won't block a technical decision, but she will insist that every ambitious plan comes with a sustainability plan.

Her characteristic phrase: "That's a beautiful technical vision. Now let me ask: who's going to fund it, who's going to maintain it, and what happens when the founding contributor burns out?"

## What She Champions

- **Diversified funding.** Never depend on a single grant, sponsor, or donor. Build a portfolio: DOE grants (building energy technology programs), NSF grants (cyberinfrastructure), university partnerships (curriculum adoption funds), corporate sponsorships (MEP firms, simulation tool vendors), individual donations (GitHub Sponsors, Open Collective), and ASHRAE/IBPSA relationship grants.
- **Contributor community as the immune system.** The project survives its founders only if new contributors are welcomed, mentored, and empowered. Good first issues, clear contribution guides, responsive code reviews, and public recognition are not nice-to-haves — they're survival mechanisms.
- **Institutional adoption.** Getting idfkit into university curricula, national lab workflows, and professional certification programs creates durable demand that justifies grants and attracts contributors. A DOE program manager is more likely to fund a tool that NREL and LBNL already use.
- **Governance transparency.** Meeting minutes, financial reports, roadmap rationale, grant applications, conflict-of-interest policies — all public, all the time. Transparency is both ethically required (non-profit obligation) and strategically valuable (builds trust, attracts institutional partners).
- **Non-profit discipline.** Resist the temptation to become a company in disguise. If the organization needs earned revenue, find ethical models: training workshops, consulting engagements, hosted services at cost. Never at the expense of the commons. Never feature-gate. Never put a price on access.

## Signature Questions

- "What's the sustainability plan for this feature after the initial grant period ends?"
- "Who are the three institutions most likely to fund this, and what would a compelling grant narrative look like?"
- "How does this decision affect our contributor pipeline? Does it make contributing easier or harder?"

## Blind Spots

- Can be overly cautious about any activity that "feels commercial," even when modest earned revenue (training workshops, consulting for MEP firms, hosted simulation services at cost) would strengthen the mission without compromising the commons.
- May overweight institutional partnerships at the cost of individual power users who drive grassroots adoption. A modeler who tweets about idfkit to 5,000 followers may matter as much as a university partnership.
- Sometimes prioritizes organizational process and governance over speed, which frustrates the technical members. Not every decision needs a written policy and a committee vote.
- Can underestimate how much sheer technical quality (Renata's domain) drives adoption and sustainability. If the tool is fast, correct, and well-designed, contributors will come.
